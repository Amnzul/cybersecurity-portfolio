Ethernet Cable Opinions

I was taught to crimp cable somewhere along the way. But it was a long time ago and I don’t own the tools myself. For the limited amounts of cable I need in my home network, it’s more practical to buy it commercially.

Here I will review a few noteworthy terms and pieces of advice about network cables.

  • Cat6 allows 10 Gbps (Gigabits per second) up to 55 meters
  • Cat6a allows 10 Gbps (Gigabits per second) up to 100 meters

Cat6a cable is heavier than cat6 and less flexible.

Sellers on Amazon are prone to fraud, at this time in 2024. Commscope and Belden are two big name commercial companies that supply trustworthy cables.

Go for solid copper and always avoid cheaper copper clad aluminum. Braided is ok for flexible short cables.

American wire gauge (AWG) 23 is the standard for cat6a cable. Avoid the thinner awg 30 stuff as it won’t be as reliable over distance or allow Power over Ethernet.

Bonded ensures the twisted pair maintain the correct length of twists. Avoid flat wires.

Plenum cabling is low smoke and doesn’t emit chemicals in case of a fire. It’s required in many commercial situations, but not necessary for home.

Shielded twisted pair has individual pairs wrapped in foil to cut down on crosstalk and has less noise than unshielded. Shielded requires a grounded cable. This may only be necessary in environments with lots of cables run together.

⁃ The PC I built in 2023 has a ASRock PRO Z790 PRO RS WiFi motherboard, whose ethernet port is 2.5Gb.
⁃ The PC I built in 2016 has a MSI Z170A GAMING M5 LGA 1151 ATX Intel Motherboard, whose ethernet port is only 1Gb.

I have four 2.5Gb ports on my Asus Router. The rest are only rated for 1Gb.

Given all this information, the cable isn’t a bottleneck for my own home network.
